RavenStack — LTV & Reactivation Analysis

Account-level lifetime value, retention, and win-back performance across 500 B2B SaaS accounts, built as a working sample of a Global Reactivations analytics workflow — KPI framing, channel/cohort LTV, survival curves, and churn-driver diagnostics.

Executive summaryThe reactivation opportunity

RavenStack carries a 22% lifetime churn rate, with the median account churning around month 9–10 of its lifecycle — half the base is gone by then on the current survival curve. That alone would be a retention story. It isn't the whole story: 277 of the 390 currently-active accounts (71%) have a past churn event on record. Most of today's revenue base is not accounts that never left — it's accounts that were won back. Reactivation isn't a side motion here; it's already the primary engine of the active book.

Acquisition channel performance splits on more than one axis. Ads-sourced accounts churn fastest (23.5%) but also reactivate at more than 4x the rate of organic or referral-driven accounts (21.7% vs ~5%) and carry the highest realized LTV of any channel — the volatility is real, but so is the recoverability. Partner and organic channels churn less but almost never come back once they leave, which argues for a different treatment: retention investment pre-churn rather than win-back spend post-churn.

Churn reasons split roughly evenly across features, budget, support, competitor, and pricing — no single fixable driver dominates, which is itself the finding: a one-size win-back offer will underperform a segmented one. Accounts citing competitor and features carry the highest historical LTV, making them the highest-value reactivation targets per dollar of campaign spend.

Applying real cost signals — support-ticket hours and refund/credit amounts — plus an industry-benchmark gross margin, net LTV comes in at $42.2M against $57.3M in gross realized revenue (74%). Critically, the channel ranking is unchanged after this adjustment: ads and partner still lead on value, event still lags. The reactivation conclusions above hold under a margin-adjusted view, not just a top-line one.

RecommendationsWhere this points the reactivation program

  1. Fund a channel-specific win-back motion, not a blanket one. Ads-sourced churners reactivate 4–5x more readily than organic/partner churners — a paid win-back offer on ads-sourced accounts should have the best payback of any channel in the portfolio.
  2. Segment win-back messaging by reason code. Competitor- and feature-driven churn carries the highest account value; a feature-gap-specific offer will outperform a generic discount for that segment, while budget/pricing churners need a commercial motion instead.
  3. Move the intervention earlier for partner/organic accounts. These channels churn less but almost never come back — the ROI is in pre-churn retention triggers (usage health scores, proactive CSM outreach), not post-churn win-back spend.
  4. Treat reactivated accounts as full-value, not discounted risk. Serial churn-and-reactivate accounts land within 10% of never-churned LTV — sizing win-back campaign budgets against a "damaged" assumption is leaving money on the table.
  5. Deal size at signup, not early behavior, is the strongest available LTV signal. Seats and initial MRR dominate the predictive model; support and satisfaction signals in the first 90 days carry almost no weight. Practically: CS/onboarding attention is better targeted by contract size at close than by waiting to see who "engages well" early on.
  6. Stop asking "will they come back" — ask "are they overdue." 87% of churn events resolve into a new subscription within weeks regardless of segment, so a binary win-back-or-not model has nothing to predict. The useful trigger is an account that's already past its reason-code-typical return window — that's a concrete, automatable signal for when to escalate from a low-cost nudge to a real offer.
  7. Budget-cycle churn needs patience, not urgency. Budget-driven churners take the longest to return (median 21 days vs. 12 for pricing) — a fast aggressive win-back push is more likely to be premature than well-timed for this reason code specifically.

Source: RavenStack synthetic SaaS dataset (accounts, subscriptions, feature_usage, support_tickets, churn_events) — dataset author River @ Rivalytics, used under its MIT-like license for portfolio/educational purposes. Realized LTV = Σ(mrr_amount × active months) per account through 2024-12-31. Projected LTV = ARPA ÷ monthly churn rate (churned accounts' average tenure), a standard SaaS approximation, not a cohort-fit model. Net LTV = realized LTV × an assumed 78% gross margin, minus actual refund/credit amounts and support cost-to-serve (ticket hours × an assumed $35/hr) — the dataset has no COGS/hosting/salary fields, so the margin % and hourly rate are disclosed industry-benchmark assumptions, not measurements. Analysis built as a working sample against the Staff Marketing Analyst (Global Reactivations) role scope — not RavenStack's actual business.
